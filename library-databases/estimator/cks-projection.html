Take-home: revenue is not the story, net income is

In a 1099-labor passthrough business, most of every billed dollar leaves as crew pay and materials. Below is the P&L under two models. Note the shape in both: fixed overhead drags Year 0 to a loss, then net margin climbs every year as revenue dilutes that fixed cost — classic operating leverage — until the market-size ceiling caps it around Year 6.

MODEL A GC / full-ticket — CKS bills the whole job, pays crew + materials out of it

MODEL B Marketplace — CKS takes an 18% fee on job value; crew & materials paid around it

Same take-home order of magnitude either way (~$860K vs ~$525K at ceiling). GC posts higher absolute net on a much bigger revenue base; the marketplace posts a higher percentage margin (38%) because CKS never touches the crew/material cost — it only ever books the fee. Which one to run is an operations question (do you want to carry payroll and materials risk?), not a profit question.

Sensitivity — the crew-payout assumption dominates

Net margin swings hard on what the 1099 crew keeps. At a 48% payout + 20% materials → ~11% net. If crews command 55% + 25% materials (tight-labor pricing power), gross falls to ~20% and net compresses to ~4–6% — i.e. ~$300–450K at the same ceiling. The friction scenario (deportation fear → scarcer crews) pushes payout up, so it hits margin as well as capacity.

△ When does it actually become impossible?

Not within this 10-year path as modeled — the market-size ceiling (SOM) caps revenue at ~$7.6M/yr around Year 6 before the labor pool is fully exhausted; under friction, the roster sits at ~75% of available crews (tight, not impossible).

The wall only appears if CKS breaks past the ~25% share ceiling — e.g. by displacing competitors faster than modeled. Stress-testing an unlocked 60% obtainable share: the friction-scenario roster meets available crews in Year 8 (~180 accounts, ~$10.2M revenue, needing 68 crews against 68 reachable). That is the genuine labor wall — it is reached only in a share-dominance scenario, not the base case. With the earlier placeholder firm count it landed a year sooner (Year 7); the real Reference Solutions count of 398 metro firms buys ~one more year of runway.

How to think about this business — a mental model

One line to carry: CKS is not a flooring company — it's a reliability-and-coordination layer sitting between fragmented property managers (who need unit turns done fast and predictably) and ~400 fragmented local crews (who are variable, and now spooked by enforcement). The product PMs pay for is "one call, floor gets done, on schedule." Everything below follows from that.

The constraint stack — what actually caps the business, in order

1. Sales / execution — can you sign & service accounts?soft, internal
2. Market size (SOM) — obtainable PM-turn niche in one metro~$7.6M ← binds
3. Labor pool — reachable crews (only bites if you break #2)68–119 crews

The one that binds is #2, market size — not labor. You run out of market before you run out of crews. That single fact reorders every priority below.

The economic engine — thin spread, high operating leverage

Gross margin is structurally fixed (~32%)crew+materials = 68%
Below ~$1.5M revenue you lose money (fixed overhead)breakeven Yr 1
Above it, each extra $1 adds ~22¢ pre-tax contribution (~16¢ after tax)→ margin climbs

You don't make money per job — you make it on volume × reliability, like a staffing agency. The flywheel: more crews → more reliability → more PMs → more crews. Density is the moat.

◆ How big is the reliability premium? — anchored to Orlando vacancy economics

Why a PM pays CKS a premium instead of coordinating crews itself: the alternative's real cost is vacancy — every day a turned unit can't be re-rented is lost rent.

STATISTAOrlando avg apartment rent (Jan 2026)$1,502/mo → ~$49/day
STATISTAOrlando-Kissimmee-Sanford 2BR fair-market rent (2025)$1,958/mo → ~$64/day
A no-show that slips a turn 3–5 days costs the PM…~$150–320 / unit

That recovered rent alone — before counting the PM's coordination time — justifies a $150–320 premium per job. The marketplace fee ($360 on a $2,000 job) and the GC markup (~$500 over direct crew price) sit right at or above that line, so the premium pays for itself on vacancy avoidance for any unit where turn-time is genuinely at risk. And Orlando 2BR rent is up 48% since 2021 — rising rents make fast turns worth more every year, a structural tailwind for CKS's value proposition.

◆ Sales or labor — where to focus?

Neither in the naïve sense. You have enough crews (398 firms; you need ~40–68) and you can sign enough accounts to hit the SOM cap. The real levers, in sequence:

Now (below ~$1.5M, near breakeven) → SALESget over the fixed-cost hump fast
Scaling up (the real game) → LABOR reliability, not headcountno-show/churn is the enemy
At the ceiling → neither — REPLICATE into a new metro+$7.6M / +$860K each

Why reliability over raw labor supply: crew show-up — not crew count — is literally the product a PM pays a premium for, and it's what the model's churn/no-show terms quietly destroy. The deportation-fear friction attacks exactly this (won't-travel, no-show), so investing in crew loyalty — steady work, fast guaranteed pay, local routing — is simultaneously a reliability play and a margin play. Chasing more salespeople past the SOM cap, or more crews past the ~68 you need, both waste money on a constraint that isn't binding.
